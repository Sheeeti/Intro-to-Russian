Russian Conjugation Rules

Present Tense:

For verbs ending in -ть, remove -ть and add the following endings:

  • я (I) -ю / -у (ya -yu / -u)
  • ты (you) -ешь (ty -esh')
  • он/она/оно (he/she/it) -ет (on/ona/ono -et)
  • мы (we) -ем (my -em)
  • вы (you, formal/plural) -ете (vy -ete)
  • они (they) -ют / -ут (oni -yut / -ut)

Example: работать (to work)

  • я работаю (I work) - ya rabotayu
  • ты работаешь (you work) - ty rabotayesh'
  • он/она/оно работает (he/she/it works) - on/ona/ono rabotayet
  • мы работаем (we work) - my rabotayem
  • вы работаете (you work) - vy rabotayete
  • они работают (they work) - oni rabotayut

Past Tense:

For verbs ending in -ть, remove -ть and add the following endings based on gender and number:

  • Masculine singular -л (l)
  • Feminine singular -ла (la)
  • Neuter singular -ло (lo)
  • Plural -ли (li)

Example: работать (to work)

  • работал (he worked) - rabotal
  • работала (she worked) - rabotala
  • работало (it worked) - rabotalo
  • работали (they worked) - rabotali

Future Tense:

There are two types of future tense: simple future and compound future.

Simple Future (Perfective verbs): Conjugate as if it were the present tense.

Example: сделать (to do)

  • я сделаю (I will do) - ya sdelayu
  • ты сделаешь (you will do) - ty sdelayesh'
  • он/она/оно сделает (he/she/it will do) - on/ona/ono sdelayet
  • мы сделаем (we will do) - my sdelayem
  • вы сделаете (you will do) - vy sdelayete
  • они сделают (they will do) - oni sdelayut

Compound Future (Imperfective verbs): Use the present tense of "быть" (to be) + infinitive of the verb.

Example: работать (to work)

  • я буду работать (I will work) - ya budu rabotat'
  • ты будешь работать (you will work) - ty budesh' rabotat'
  • он/она/оно будет работать (he/she/it will work) - on/ona/ono budet rabotat'
  • мы будем работать (we will work) - my budem rabotat'
  • вы будете работать (you will work) - vy budete rabotat'
  • они будут работать (they will work) - oni budut rabotat'

Russian History

Russian history spans thousands of years, from the early Slavic tribes to the formation of the Russian state and beyond. Here are some key periods:

  • Kievan Rus' (882–1240): The early medieval state that was the precursor to modern Russia, Ukraine, and Belarus.
  • Mongol Invasion (1237–1480): The period of Mongol domination over the Russian principalities.
  • Muscovy (1283–1547): The rise of the Grand Duchy of Moscow, which became the dominant Russian principality.
  • Tsardom of Russia (1547–1721): The period when Russia was ruled by tsars, beginning with Ivan the Terrible.
  • Russian Empire (1721–1917): The era of imperial expansion and consolidation, starting with Peter the Great and ending with the Russian Revolution.
  • Soviet Union (1922–1991): The period of communist rule, from the aftermath of the Russian Revolution to the dissolution of the USSR.
  • Russian Federation (1991–present): The modern state of Russia, following the collapse of the Soviet Union.

Sentence Structure Rules

Russian sentence structure can vary, but here are some basic rules:

  • Subject-Verb-Object (SVO): This is the most common word order. For example: Я люблю тебя (Ya lyublyu tebya) - I love you.
  • Questions: Questions often have the same word order as statements but are indicated by intonation. For example: Ты любишь меня? (Ty lyubish' menya?) - Do you love me?
  • Negations: Negations are formed by placing "не" before the verb. For example: Я не люблю (Ya ne lyublyu) - I don't love.
  • Adjectives: Adjectives usually come before the nouns they modify. For example: красивая девушка (krasivaya devushka) - beautiful girl.
  • Prepositions: Prepositions come before their objects. For example: на столе (na stole) - on the table.
